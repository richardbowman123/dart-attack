# Dart Attack - Game Narrative Design Document

## The Premise

You're sixteen years old. Skinny as a rake. You've snuck into the back room of a pub with a pint of lemonade and a set of brass darts your grandad left you. The dartboard on the wall is held up with gaffer tape. Nobody in here thinks you'll amount to anything.

They're wrong.

This is the story of how you went from pub nobody to world champion — by any means necessary.

---

## Character Selection

At the start of the game, the player picks one of four characters. Each is a stereotype played for laughs, with a distinct visual identity and darts nickname. Every nickname doubles as their inflatable — this is by design.

### Option A: Dai "The Dragon" Davies
- **Origin:** Valleys, South Wales
- **Look:** Skinny, wispy moustache he's clearly proud of, rugby shirt two sizes too big
- **Personality:** Quietly determined, dry humour, says "tidy" after a good throw
- **Starting pub:** The Red Dragon, Pontypridd
- **Crowd chant:** "Dai! Dai! Dai!"
- **Inflatable:** Inflatable dragon (red, wings out, fire-breathing)

### Option B: Terry "The Hammer" Hoskins
- **Origin:** East London
- **Look:** Shaved head, earring, Stone Island polo, built like a whippet
- **Personality:** Mouthy, cocky, points at the board before every throw like he's already won
- **Starting pub:** The Blind Beggar, Bethnal Green
- **Crowd chant:** "HAM-MER! HAM-MER!"
- **Inflatable:** Inflatable hammer (massive, bright yellow, oversized head)

### Option C: Rab "The Flame" McTavish
- **Origin:** Dundee, Scotland
- **Look:** Shock of ginger hair, pale as milk, wears a kilt to every match from county level onwards
- **Personality:** Wild energy, celebrates every treble like he's scored at Hampden, wind-up merchant
- **Starting pub:** The Braw Lad, Dundee
- **Crowd chant:** "FLAME ON! FLAME ON!"
- **Inflatable:** Inflatable flame/torch (orange and red, flickering shape)

### Option D: Siobhan "The Banshee" O'Hara
- **Origin:** Belfast, Northern Ireland
- **Look:** Blonde hair scraped back tight, hoop earrings, leopard print top, sharp eyes
- **Personality:** Fearless, talks trash with a smile, blows kisses to the crowd after a big finish. Calls every male opponent "love" in a way that's somehow both friendly and terrifying.
- **Starting pub:** The Crown Bar, Belfast
- **Crowd chant:** "BAN-SHEE! BAN-SHEE!" (with a wailing sound effect)
- **Inflatable:** Inflatable banshee/ghost (white, screaming face, flowing hair)

All four start at the same point: sixteen, underweight, skint, unknown. The journey is the same — only the flavour changes.

## Career Progression - The Levels

### Level 1: Round the Clock — The Back Room
**Format:** Round the Clock (hit 1 through 20 in order, then bull to finish)
**Setting:** Back room of your local pub. Sticky carpet. Fruit machine flashing in the corner. Three regulars watching.
**Opponent:** "Big Kev" — a 50-year-old regular who's played every Thursday for 30 years. Beer belly, reading glasses pushed up on his head, cargo shorts regardless of weather. Patronising. Calls you "son" and shakes his head after your bad throws.
**Purpose:** Tutorial level. Teaches the player the throw mechanic, aiming, and board layout without score pressure. No bust rules, no doubles — just hit the numbers in order.
**Narrative beat:** When you win, Big Kev buys you a half-pint of lager (your first) and says "Right then. You might not be completely useless."
**Unlock:** Entry to the Friday Night Tournament.

### Level 2: Friday Night Tournament — 101
**Format:** 101 (start at 101, must finish on a double)
**Setting:** Same pub, but it's Friday night. The tables have been pushed back. There's a chalked bracket on a blackboard. Twenty players. Pint glasses everywhere. Someone's brought a portable speaker playing "Chase the Sun."
**Opponent (Final):** "Dangerous" Derek Marsh — mid-30s, sleeve tattoos, takes it far too seriously for a pub tournament. Wears a custom polo shirt with his own face on it. Stares you down between throws.
**Purpose:** Introduces the double-finish rule and shorter format. The player learns that finishing is a skill — you can't just throw randomly.
**Narrative beat:** Winning the Friday night earns you a trophy (a plastic dart stuck to a block of wood) and the attention of a man in the corner who says he "knows people in the regional circuit."
**Unlock:** Regional Pub Tournament entry + first manager choice.

### Level 3: Regional Pub Tournament — 301
**Format:** 301 (single leg, double to finish)
**Setting:** A social club function room. Proper oche line taped on the floor. A small stage with the board. Folding chairs for fifty spectators. A commentator with a microphone who gets names wrong.
**Opponent (Final):** "Slippery" Steve Nolan — a wiry bloke who talks constantly during your throw. Coughs at the wrong moment. Stands too close. The crowd loves him because he's "entertainment." He's been regional champion three years running.
**Purpose:** Longer format means the player needs consistency, not just a hot streak. Managing a 301 game requires thinking about what finishes are available.
**Narrative beat:** Steve tries to shake your hand before you've finished your celebration. The commentator calls you "a real prospect." Local paper takes your photo (they spell your name wrong).
**Unlock:** County Tournament entry + inflatable investment opportunity.

### Level 4: County Tournament — 501
**Format:** 501 (single leg, double to finish)
**Setting:** A civic hall. Proper lighting rig above the board. A raised oche. Banners for sponsors (a bathroom fittings company and a local kebab shop). Two hundred people in the crowd. TV cameras from a regional channel.
**Opponent (Final):** "The Professor" Philip Hartley — glasses, cardigan, looks like he should be doing a crossword. But he's surgically precise. Barely celebrates. Just nods and collects his darts. The scariest kind of opponent — one who never seems bothered.
**Purpose:** Full 501 format. The player now needs the complete skill set: big scoring, setup shots, and clinical finishing. This is where the game stops being friendly.
**Narrative beat:** After you beat The Professor, he shakes your hand properly and says "You'll do." Your manager gets a call. National qualifying has a spot with your name on it.
**Unlock:** National Qualifying + betting syndicate opportunity.

### Level 5: National Qualifying — 501
**Format:** 501 (single leg, double to finish)
**Setting:** A conference centre in Milton Keynes. Harsh fluorescent lighting. The oche is professional standard. Five hundred people, most of them other players watching from the crowd. The atmosphere is tense — everyone here thinks they're good enough.
**Opponent (Final):** "Mad Dog" Micky Burns — shaved head, neck tattoo of a bulldog, arrives to his own entrance music (a dubstep remix of "Eye of the Tiger"). Throws with terrifying power. His darts hit the board so hard they vibrate. The crowd is split — half love him, half are scared of him.
**Purpose:** This is the gatekeeper level. The difficulty spike. Mad Dog scores heavily and the player needs to keep pace. Introduces pressure — if you lose here, the World Championship dream is over.
**Narrative beat:** You survive. Just. Your checkout is tight. Mad Dog kicks a chair on the way out. Your manager says "Right. Palace time." You're going to the Worlds.
**Unlock:** World Championship.

### Level 6: World Championship — Best of Three Legs of 501
**Format:** Best of three legs, 501, double to finish
**Setting:** Alexandra Palace, London. The cathedral of darts. Walk-on music. Pyrotechnics. A baying crowd in fancy dress. The board is lit like a shrine. Two thousand people singing your name (or booing it).
**Opponent (Semi-Final):** "The Viking" Lars Eriksson — a 6'5" Scandinavian with a braided beard and themed entrance (horned helmet, dry ice). Methodical. Averages 95+. The crowd adore him. He's the sentimental favourite.
**Opponent (Final):** "The Showman" Vinnie Gold — reigning world champion. Gold shirt, gold chain, gold darts. Walk-on involves actual fireworks. He's won three titles. He's arrogant because he can afford to be. Points at the treble 20 before throwing — and hits it. He's the final boss. Everything you've built towards.
**Purpose:** First multi-leg match. The player must win 2 out of 3 legs. This tests stamina, nerve, and the ability to recover from losing a leg. All the meta-game investments (weight, drink, jewellery, manager) pay off here.
**Narrative beat:** Winning the final triggers the full ending sequence (see Ending below).

## Game Over — How You Lose (or Die)

The game ends in one of two ways: you fail on the oche, or you fail off it.

### Three Strikes and You're Out

If you lose the same level three times in a row, your career is over. You weren't good enough. The game doesn't offer infinite retries — this is a career, and careers end.

**What happens:**
- First loss: "Tough night. Shake it off. You'll get another crack." Your manager is supportive.
- Second loss: "Two in a row. People are starting to talk." The crowd is thinner next time. Commentary is less kind. Your manager looks worried.
- Third loss: Game over. A slow fade. The commentator says "And that, I'm afraid, is that." Cut to your character back in the pub where they started, watching someone else play on the board. Big Kev puts a hand on your shoulder. "Unlucky, son."

The three-strike counter resets when you win a level and progress.

### Death

The player can die. This is permanent game over — no continues, no second chances. Death can strike between rounds or, more dramatically, mid-match. There are three causes, each tied to the player's meta-game choices.

#### Liver Failure (from drinking)
**Trigger:** Cumulative drinking across the whole game. Every drink the player has — between matches AND during matches — adds to a hidden "liver damage" meter. There's no visible warning until it's nearly too late.
**Warning signs (in order):**
1. "You're looking a bit yellow, love" — a barmaid's comment between matches. Easy to miss.
2. Your character's skin tone shifts slightly towards yellow in the between-match screens.
3. A sharp pain during a match — the screen flashes and your character winces. The commentator says "Something's not right here."
**Death trigger:** If the liver meter fills completely, it can strike between rounds (a hospital cutscene — "The doctors say your liver's packed in. You gave it everything. Including your liver.") or mid-match (you collapse at the oche — the crowd goes silent, St John Ambulance rush in, fade to black).

#### Heart Attack (from overeating)
**Trigger:** Cumulative weight gain combined with match stress. The heavier you are AND the more nervous you are during high-pressure moments, the higher the risk.
**Warning signs (in order):**
1. Shortness of breath — between matches, your character is seen panting on stairs.
2. A chest twinge during a match — your throw is interrupted by a brief clutch at the chest. "Just indigestion" your character says.
3. Your manager says "Maybe ease off the buffets, eh?"
**Death trigger:** If weight is at maximum AND nerves spike during a critical moment (e.g., match point at the Worlds), there's a chance the heart gives out. You clutch your chest mid-throw. The dart leaves your hand — it might even hit the target — but you're already falling. Ambulance. Fade to black. "He gave his heart to the game. Literally."

#### Gang Hit (from the betting syndicate)
**Trigger:** If you bet against yourself and then accidentally WIN (failing to throw the match), the syndicate loses money. They give you one warning. If you cross them again — by winning another match they've bet against, or by refusing to co-operate — they send someone.
**Warning signs (in order):**
1. A text message between matches: "That wasn't the arrangement."
2. Your car windscreen is smashed in the car park after a match.
3. A man in a dark coat is watching from the back of the crowd. Your manager says "Don't look at him."
**Death trigger:** Between rounds, a cutscene. You're walking to your car. Footsteps behind you. Fade to black. A newspaper headline: "DARTS STAR FOUND IN CAR PARK." Your manager is "unavailable for comment."

### The Doctor (Health Check & Medicine)

The player's health meters (liver damage, heart risk) are completely hidden. You never see a bar or a number. The ONLY way to find out how you're doing is to pay for a doctor's visit between matches.

**How it works:**
- Between any two levels, the player can visit the doctor (costs money)
- The doctor gives you a plain-English verdict — not a number, a conversation:
  - "You're in fine shape. Whatever you're doing, keep it up."
  - "Your liver's working a bit hard. Might want to ease off the pints."
  - "I'm going to be honest with you — your liver is in trouble. Serious trouble."
  - "Your heart's under strain. At your weight, I'd be very careful about high-stress situations."
  - "I'm not going to sugarcoat it. If you don't make changes, you won't see the final."
- The doctor never gives exact numbers — just honest, increasingly alarming assessments

**Medicine:**
- If the doctor flags a problem, the player can pay for medicine
- Medicine is expensive — it competes with food, jewellery, darts, and inflatables for your money
- **Liver medicine:** Reduces the liver damage meter by a chunk. Doesn't reset it — just buys you more runway. The doctor says "This'll help, but it's not a licence to drink yourself daft."
- **Heart medicine:** Reduces heart risk. The doctor says "Beta blockers. They'll take the edge off. But you still need to watch the weight."
- Medicine is available every time you visit the doctor, but each successive course costs more (diminishing returns — your body builds tolerance)
- You can visit the doctor multiple times across the game, but each visit costs money. Do you spend your cash on medicine or on tungsten darts?

**The tension:** The player who drinks heavily and eats everything might feel fine — no warnings, no symptoms — right up until the barmaid says "You're looking yellow." Then they're scrambling to find money for a doctor's visit and medicine, money they might have spent on inflatables or jewellery. The player who visits the doctor regularly stays informed but burns cash. It's another trade-off.

**Narrative flavour:** The doctor's surgery is a grotty NHS waiting room. Fluorescent lights, out-of-date magazines, a child coughing in the corner. The doctor is tired but straightforward. If you come back too many times, he says "You again? Right, let's have a look." If you're in genuinely bad shape, there's a pause before he speaks. That pause tells you everything.

### Design Note on Death
Death should feel shocking but earned. The player made the choices that led here. It's dark comedy, not horror — the tone should be "oh no, I actually killed myself with chip butties" rather than genuinely distressing. The warning signs give the player a chance to course-correct (visit the doctor, buy medicine, drink water, eat less, stay away from the syndicate). Death is avoidable if you pay attention — and if you're willing to spend the money.

## The Nerve-O-Metre

A visible meter on screen during every match. This is the core in-game tension mechanic.

### How it works

The Nerve-O-Metre sits on the HUD — a vertical bar that fluctuates between CALM (bottom, green) and TERRIFIED (top, red).

**What nerves do to your throw:**
- **Calm:** Minimal scatter. The reticle is steady. You throw where you aim.
- **Slightly nervous:** Small random wobble on the reticle. Throws land close to target but not dead-on.
- **Nervous:** Noticeable wobble. The reticle drifts. Treble 20 becomes "somewhere near 20."
- **Very nervous:** Significant drift. The reticle wanders. You might be aiming at 20 and hit 5 or 1.
- **Terrified:** Chaotic wobble. The reticle bounces. Anything could happen. Misses become common.

### What raises nerves
- **Start of match:** Nerves start at a baseline set by the level (pub = low, Worlds = very high)
- **Missing a shot you needed:** Nerves tick up slightly
- **Opponent scoring well:** Their big scores push your nerves up
- **Match point against you:** Spike
- **Crowd hostility:** If the crowd is against you (low bling, no inflatables), nerves rise faster
- **Being close to checkout:** The closer you get to finishing, the more the pressure builds
- **Sobriety:** Being completely sober means your baseline nerves are higher (see Drinking below)

### What lowers nerves
- **Hitting a good score:** A 140 or 180 brings nerves down noticeably
- **Drinking:** The primary nerve-reducer (see below)
- **Crowd support:** High bling and inflatables mean the crowd lifts you — slower nerve build-up
- **Manager pep talk:** If you have Sue the Coach, she gives a nerve-reducing pep talk between legs
- **Opponent missing:** When they miss a double or bust, your nerves drop
- **Experience:** At higher levels, your baseline nerve management is slightly better (you've been here before)

### Nerve-O-Metre and the final dart
At match point, when you're on a double to win, the Nerve-O-Metre should be front and centre. The wobble is at its worst. This is where drinking management pays off — if you're at the sweet spot (1-2 pints), your nerves are manageable. Too sober and you're shaking. Too drunk and you can't see. This moment IS the game.

## In-Game Drinking (During Matches)

Drinking is not just a between-match decision. The player can drink DURING the match, between visits (sets of three darts).

### How it works

After each visit (your three darts have been thrown and scored), before the next visit, a quick prompt appears:

**"Have a drink?"**
- Half pint (small nerve reduction, small blur increase)
- Full pint (moderate nerve reduction, moderate blur increase)
- Nothing (nerves stay where they are, vision stays clear)

This is a quick tap — not a menu that breaks the flow. It should feel like reaching for your glass on the oche shelf, which is exactly what real darts players do.

### The visual effects of drinking

**Blur levels (cumulative across the match):**
- **Sober (0 drinks):** Crystal clear board. Sharp edges. Perfect visibility. But the Nerve-O-Metre baseline is higher.
- **Mellow (1-2 halves / 1 pint):** No visible change. Nerves are noticeably calmer. This is the sweet spot.
- **Warm (2-3 pints):** Very slight softening at the edges of the board. Barely noticeable. Nerves are low.
- **Tipsy (3-4 pints):** The board starts to blur at the edges. The numbers are slightly harder to read. The wire lines between segments soften. Nerves are very low — almost too relaxed.
- **Drunk (5-6 pints):** The board visibly blurs. The segments merge at the edges. Aiming becomes guesswork in the outer ring. Nerves are gone but so is precision.
- **Hammered (7+ pints):** Double vision. The board appears to split into two overlapping images that drift apart. The reticle wanders independently of your input. You can barely control where the dart goes. This is functionally unplayable — but hilarious.

### The double vision effect
At the "hammered" level, the dartboard literally appears doubled — two boards overlapping, slightly offset, drifting in and out of alignment. Only one is "real" (the dart hits based on the real board position) but the player can't tell which is which. This is the punishment for over-drinking and it should look comically awful.

### Drinking strategy
The player must manage their drinking across the whole match:
- **Too little:** High nerves, shaky aim, especially under pressure
- **Just right:** Calm nerves, clear vision — but you need to keep topping up as nerves rebuild
- **Too much:** No nerves but you can't see the board properly
- **Way too much:** You're playing blind and you might die of liver failure

### Drinking between matches
Between levels, the player's alcohol level resets (you've sobered up overnight). But the cumulative liver damage does NOT reset. Every drink across the entire game counts towards the liver failure threshold.

## Meta-Game Systems

These systems run between matches AND during matches. The player earns money from wins and side activities, then spends it on things that affect their stats and progression. Every investment has a trade-off.

### 1. Weight (Eating)

**The mechanic:** Your character starts skinny. Heavier players can throw heavier darts, which are more accurate (less scatter) but require more power to throw well.

**How it works:**
- Earn money > spend on food > gain weight
- Weight has tiers: Skinny > Average > Stocky > Heavy > Unit
- Each tier unlocks heavier dart sets (brass > nickel silver > tungsten > premium tungsten)
- Heavier darts have tighter scatter but need more power input to reach the board properly
- If you're too light for your darts, throws fall short

**Food options (escalating cost and calories):**
- Chip butty (cheap, small gain)
- Full English (moderate)
- Curry and chips (good gain)
- Sunday roast with seconds (big gain)
- All-you-can-eat Chinese buffet (massive gain, but you feel sluggish for the next match — temporary accuracy penalty)

**Narrative flavour:** Your character visibly fills out through the levels. The skinny teenager becomes a proper darts physique. Commentators remark on it. "Filled out nicely since the regionals, haven't they?"

### 2. Drinking (see also: In-Game Drinking and Nerve-O-Metre above)

**The mechanic:** Alcohol is the primary nerve-management tool. It steadies your nerves but clouds your vision. The player must find the sweet spot — and they manage it in real-time during matches, not just between them.

**Between matches — the drinks menu:**
- Half pint of lager (cheap, small nerve prep)
- Pint of lager (standard)
- Pint of Guinness (stronger steadying effect, heavier on blur, more liver damage)
- Double whisky (fast-acting, big nerve drop, big blur, serious liver hit)
- Water (sobers you up — but from Level 4 onwards, you get booed by the crowd)

**During matches — between visits (every 3 darts):**
- Half pint or full pint, quick tap decision (see In-Game Drinking section above)
- This is where the real management happens — topping up nerve reduction while watching the blur creep in

**The danger:** Every single drink across the entire game contributes to the hidden liver damage meter. The player who drinks their way through every match will play with steady nerves but might not survive to lift the trophy.

**Narrative flavour:** Early levels, nobody cares if you drink water. By the Worlds, the crowd expects you to have a pint on the oche. Drinking water gets genuine boos. The commentator says "Interesting choice" in a disapproving tone.

### 3. Jewellery (Crowd Respect)

**The mechanic:** The darts world runs on showmanship. Jewellery earns crowd respect, which translates to louder cheers that boost your confidence (slight accuracy bonus). But too much weighs down your throwing arm.

**How it works:**
- Jewellery has a "bling level" and a "weight penalty"
- Low bling: crowd is lukewarm — no confidence bonus
- Medium bling: crowd is engaged — small accuracy bonus from atmosphere
- High bling: crowd goes wild — bigger bonus, but arm weight starts to drag
- Excessive bling: crowd loves you but your throwing arm is so heavy that power drops and scatter increases

**Jewellery items (cumulative):**
- Sovereign ring (small bling, negligible weight)
- Gold chain (moderate bling, small weight)
- Chunky bracelet (good bling, moderate weight)
- Diamond-encrusted watch (high bling, significant weight)
- Full Mr T starter kit — multiple chains, rings on every finger (maximum bling, severe weight penalty)

**Narrative flavour:** Your character's appearance evolves. By the Worlds, the walk-on is a spectacle. The commentator lists your jewellery like a shopping channel. "And here he comes — the sovereign rings, the chains, is that a NEW bracelet?"

### 4. The Manager

**The mechanic:** After Level 2, you must choose a manager. This is a permanent choice that shapes the rest of the game. Each manager has a distinct personality and provides different bonuses.

**Option A: "Big Phil" Carver — The Money Man**
- **Look:** Loud suit, cigar, car salesman energy
- **Bonus:** +30% prize money and sponsorship earnings at every level
- **Downside:** Doesn't care about your game. No training benefits. "Just win, son. The rest is my department."
- **Special ability:** Negotiates better inflatable deals (see below)
- **Best for:** Players who want to invest heavily in meta-game systems (eating, jewellery, better darts)

**Option B: "Silent" Sue Palmer — The Coach**
- **Look:** Tracksuit, clipboard, reading glasses on a chain, no-nonsense
- **Bonus:** -15% scatter on all throws (she drills you relentlessly between matches)
- **Downside:** Takes a bigger cut of prize money. "I'm not cheap, but I'm worth it."
- **Special ability:** Gives you a pre-match pep talk that temporarily reduces the sober hand-shake penalty
- **Best for:** Players who want pure accuracy improvement

**Option C: "Dodgy" Dave Slater — The Fixer**
- **Look:** Leather jacket, always on his phone, knows everyone
- **Bonus:** Can intimidate opponents — their scatter increases slightly when Dave is in their eyeline
- **Downside:** Occasionally "borrows" money from your winnings. You'll sometimes find your purse lighter than expected.
- **Special ability:** Arranges the bribe (see Level 3 event) at a discount. Also gets better odds from bookmakers for the betting syndicate.
- **Best for:** Players who want to win through disruption and the corruption path

### 5. The Bribe (One-Time Event — Level 3)

**When:** Before the Regional final against Slippery Steve.

**The setup:** Your manager pulls you aside. "Look. Steve's been regional champ three years running. But he's also skint. His missus has left him. He owes money to the wrong people. For the right price... he might have an off night."

**The choice:**
- **Pay the bribe** — costs a significant chunk of your savings. Steve throws badly in the final. You win comfortably but the crowd is confused. One journalist in the corner is taking notes. (This has consequences — see Reputation below.)
- **Refuse the bribe** — you play Steve at full strength. Harder match, but clean win. The crowd respects you more. (Slight permanent crowd bonus.)

**Narrative flavour:** If you bribed Steve, there's a moment at the county tournament where someone in the crowd holds up a sign: "WE KNOW ABOUT STEVE." It doesn't affect gameplay but it's unsettling.

### 6. The Inflatable (Investment — from Level 3 onwards)

**When:** After winning the Regional, you're offered the chance to invest in an inflatable.

**The setup:** A man with a van approaches you in the car park. "You're getting a following, son. People need something to wave. I can get you inflatables — custom job, your face on it, any shape you want."

**The choice:** Your inflatable is tied to your nickname (set at character select):
- Dai "The Dragon" — inflatable dragon
- Terry "The Hammer" — inflatable hammer
- Rab "The Flame" — inflatable flame/torch
- Siobhan "The Banshee" — inflatable banshee

**How it works:**
- You invest upfront in a batch of inflatables
- At subsequent matches, a percentage of the crowd buys them
- Bigger crowds = more sales = more money
- At the Worlds (2,000 crowd), this becomes your biggest income stream
- The money funds your final preparations (best darts, optimal weight, perfect jewellery loadout)

**Narrative flavour:** The inflatables become part of your identity. The commentator references them. "The crowd is a sea of inflatable dragons tonight!" By the Worlds, there's a moment where you see a kid in the crowd holding your inflatable, wide-eyed. A flash of the person you used to be.

### 7. The Betting Syndicate (from Level 4 onwards)

**When:** After the County Tournament, Dave (or your manager, if not Dave) introduces you to "some lads who like a flutter."

**The setup:** "Here's the thing. You KNOW you're going to win. So why not make money on it? And sometimes... sometimes it's smarter to know you're going to LOSE."

**How it works:**
- Before each match from Level 4 onwards, you can place a bet
- **Bet on yourself:** Safe money if you win. Lose the bet if you lose the match.
- **Bet on your opponent:** You have to deliberately lose the match. This is a one-time option — you can only do it once, and it delays your progression by one round (you have to replay the level). But the payout is huge.
- The betting syndicate takes a cut, but the returns fund your World Championship prep

**The one-time "throw" option:**
- At ONE point (player chooses when), you can bet against yourself
- You then have to deliberately lose — but the game still plays normally, you just need to NOT finish
- This is harder than it sounds — the game doesn't have a "throw badly" button. You have to intentionally miss finishes.
- If you accidentally win while trying to lose, you lose the bet AND the syndicate is angry (temporary penalty — they rough up your practice board)

**Narrative flavour:** The moral compromise is the point. You're a kid from nowhere making it by whatever means necessary. The game never judges you — but the crowd might. Betting against yourself and losing on purpose triggers suspicious commentary: "Well that's not like him at all..."

## Reputation System (Hidden)

The player's choices accumulate into a hidden reputation score that affects the final narrative.

**Clean choices:** Refuse the bribe, never bet against yourself, pick the coach as manager
**Dirty choices:** Take the bribe, bet against yourself, pick the fixer, over-invest in bling over skill

This doesn't affect whether you can WIN — you can win the Worlds either way. It affects HOW you win and what the ending feels like.

### Opponent Scaling

Each opponent is harder than the last. This is achieved through:
- **Scoring ability:** Big Kev averages 35. Vinnie Gold averages 100+.
- **Finishing:** Early opponents miss doubles frequently. Late opponents rarely miss.
- **Pressure handling:** Early opponents crack under pressure (miss when close to finishing). Vinnie Gold gets BETTER under pressure.
- **Personality during play:** Steve talks, Micky slams darts, Philip says nothing. Each creates a different psychological atmosphere.

From the County Tournament, the player gets a walk-on. This is a short sequence where you walk to the oche with music playing and the crowd reacting.

## The World Championship — Detailed Structure

The Worlds is a three-leg match (best of three). This is the first time the player faces a multi-leg format.

### Semi-Final vs "The Viking" Lars Eriksson
**Atmosphere:** Respectful but intense. Lars has his own huge following (half the crowd in Viking helmets). The commentary team talks about his "Scandinavian precision."
**Challenge:** Lars is consistent. He won't blow up. You need to out-score him and take your doubles when they come. He averages 95 and rarely misses a double inside 20.
**Between legs:** You can drink, adjust, talk to your manager for a pep talk.

### Final vs "The Showman" Vinnie Gold
**Atmosphere:** Electric. Vinnie's walk-on lasts a full minute. Gold confetti. His face is on the big screen. Half the crowd chant his name, half chant yours. The noise is deafening.
**Challenge:** Vinnie is the complete package. He averages 100+. He hits 180s. He finishes on bull when he fancies it. But he's also arrogant — if you take the first leg convincingly, there's a crack. He starts gesturing to the crowd instead of focusing.
**Between legs:** This is where every meta-game investment comes together. Your weight determines your dart quality. Your drink level determines your steadiness. Your jewellery and inflatables determine the crowd's backing. Your manager determines your edge.
**The final dart:** If you win, the last dart freezes in slow motion. The crowd noise builds. The camera holds on the dart as it arcs through the air. It lands. The number drops to zero. Silence for one beat. Then the eruption.

## The Ending

There are six possible endings: three victory endings (based on reputation) and three death endings.

### Death Endings

#### Liver Failure
Your character collapses — either at the oche mid-throw or between rounds in the corridor. Hospital scene. The doctor shakes his head. Cut to a memorial at the pub: your darts pinned to the board, a pint left on the shelf beneath. Big Kev raises a glass. "He could throw. I'll give him that." The commentary team: "A talent taken too soon. The game is poorer tonight."

#### Heart Attack
Mid-match at a critical moment. You clutch your chest. The dart might still leave your hand — it might even land. But you're already going down. The crowd gasps. St John Ambulance are there immediately but it's too late. Your manager is at the side of the stage, ashen. Newspaper headline: "DARTS DREAM ENDS IN TRAGEDY." Big Kev, watching on telly, puts his pint down slowly.

#### Gang Hit
Between rounds. Car park. Footsteps. Fade to black. Newspaper headline: "DARTS STAR FOUND IN CAR PARK — POLICE INVESTIGATE GAMBLING LINKS." Your manager is "unavailable for comment." Your inflatable is seen deflating slowly in the gutter outside the venue. Big Kev reads the paper the next morning and says "I bloody knew it."

### Victory Endings

### The Clean Champion
If your reputation is mostly clean — you refused the bribe, didn't bet against yourself, earned your way:
- The crowd lifts you up. The commentator calls you "the people's champion."
- Your manager (whoever it is) says something genuine.
- There's a shot of the pub where you started. Big Kev is watching on the telly. He turns to the bar and says "I taught him everything he knows."
- End card: Your character holding the trophy, back in the pub, pint in hand, grinning.

### The Dirty Champion
If your reputation is mostly dirty — you bribed Steve, bet against yourself, played the system:
- You win, but the celebration feels hollow. The crowd cheers but the camera finds the journalist from the regionals, still taking notes.
- Your manager congratulates you with a handshake that's clearly about the money.
- There's a flash-forward: a newspaper headline about a darts corruption investigation. Your name is in the article. But you got away with it — for now.
- End card: Your character alone in a car park, trophy in one hand, phone in the other, looking over their shoulder.

### The Mixed Champion (most likely outcome)
If you made some clean and some dirty choices:
- You win. The crowd loves you. But there's a moment where you look into the camera and there's something behind the eyes — you know what you did.
- Your manager says "We did it the hard way, didn't we?" with a knowing look.
- End card: Your character at the oche, alone, practising. Back to basics. Whatever it took, you got here. Now it's about staying here.

### Career Over (Three Strikes)
You lost the same round three times. The dream is dead. A slow montage: packing up your darts case, the drive home, the pub door opening. You sit at the bar. Big Kev slides a pint over. "You gave it a go, son. More than most." The dartboard is right there on the wall. You look at it. You look away. End card: your character at the bar, back to the board, staring into a pint. The inflatables are in a bin bag by the door.

## Tone and Style Notes

- **Comedy, not satire.** The game loves darts. It's affectionate, not mocking. Every character is a stereotype but a loveable one.
- **The corruption is fun, not dark.** Bribing Steve feels cheeky, not criminal. The betting syndicate feels like a caper, not organised crime.
- **The player is the underdog throughout.** Even by the Worlds, you're the newcomer. Vinnie Gold is the establishment. You're the story.
- **British to the core.** Sticky carpets, chip butties, Guinness, kebab shop sponsors, Stone Island polos. This is a love letter to pub culture.
- **The commentary sells it.** A running commentary voice (think Sid Waddell meets Ray Winstone) should punctuate key moments. "They've only gone and done it! From the back room of the Red Dragon to the Palace! You couldn't write it!"
